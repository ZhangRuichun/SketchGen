‚ú® Motivation

Illustration images play a significant role in enhancing imagination, improving comprehension, and fostering engagement in storybooks.

However, manually creating high-quality, contextually appropriate, and appealing storybook illustrations can be challenging and time-consuming.

Inspired by the current advancement of powerful text-to-image generative models, we propose to leverage the capabilities of stable diffusion models to address this challenge.

Stable diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable success in synthesizing high-quality images.

By fine-tuning the stable diffusion model on a dataset of storybook illustration images in a unique sketchy style, we aim to automate the process of generating engaging illustrations tailored to storybooks and examine the factors affecting the model's quality when learning a rare style.

ü§ñ Fine-Tuning Setup

The Hugging Face Sketch dataset consists of approximately 10,000 freehand scene sketches created by 100 non-expert individuals, providing object- and scene-level abstractions.

Each sketch is augmented with a corresponding text description.

We fine-tuned our model on 4x V100 graphics cards.

We saved all the model checkpoints from training steps 1,000 to 15,000.

‚Äî how we evaluate this

üîç Evaluation Metrics

We utilize CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) and FID (Fr√©chet Inception Distance) scores to evaluate the performance of our model.

CLIP is calculated for each image and prompt pair, taking into account the rare style of our dataset.

To capture the style accurately, we employ prompt engineering during the CLIP calculation.

For instance, when using the prompt: Help the little bunny find its missing carrot to generate an image, we calculate its corresponding CLIP score using the prompt: A children's comic book sketch of "Help the little bunny find its missing carrot."

This tailored prompt ensures that the CLIP score aligns with our desired style, with a higher score indicating better alignment.

FID is calculated by comparing the distribution of our fine-tuning dataset with the distribution of generated images. A lower FID score indicates better-quality images, as it signifies a closer match between the fine-tuning dataset distribution and the distribution of generated images.

To construct our fine-tuning dataset distribution, we randomly select 500 images from the fine-tuning dataset.
‚Äî how we evaluate this

üß™ Experiment Setup

To assess the impact of training steps and inference steps on our model, we conducted four experiments.

In each experiment, our fine-tuned model generated 100 images using the same set of 100 text prompts, and we preserved the corresponding image-text pairs.

Utilizing these pairs, we computed the CLIP score. Additionally, we evaluated the image distribution by comparing it with the distribution of images in our fine-tuned dataset, which resulted in the calculation of the FID score.

The experiments encompassed the following configurations:

  • training steps = 1,000, inference steps = 30
  • training steps = 1,000, inference steps = 60
  • training steps = 15,000, inference steps = 30
  • training steps = 15,000, inference steps = 60
